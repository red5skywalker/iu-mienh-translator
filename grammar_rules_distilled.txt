## Iu Mienh Grammar Rules

### Word Order
1. Iu Mien is topic-prominent. Translate mainly as **Topic/Subject + Verb + Object/Complement**, but think in terms of **Topic → Focus**, not rigid English SVO.
2. Put background or known information earlier and newer or more important information later. The strongest focus often falls near the end of the clause.
3. Keep the main verb in the middle of the clause. Time words, topic phrases, and coverb phrases usually come before the main focused predicate.
4. Time is expressed lexically, not by verb inflection. Use time words such as *ih zanc* “now”, *zinh ndaangc* “formerly”, and *nqa’haav hingv* “later/future”.
5. Dependent clauses usually come before the main clause. A common exception is purpose, reason, and simile clauses, which may follow the main clause.

### Noun Phrases
6. The noun is usually the semantic head. Common NP patterns are **N + postnominal stative/action verb**, **V/Adj + nyei + N**, **Possessor + nyei + N**, **DEM + CLF + N**, and **NUM + CLF + N**.
7. Many adjectival meanings are verbal in Iu Mien. Use **N + V_ADJ** or **V_ADJ + nyei + N** rather than forcing an English-style adjective before the noun.
8. Use **nyei** for possession: **Possessor + nyei + N**. Example: *yie nyei biauv* “my house”.
9. Use **nyei** for restrictive relative clauses: **Clause + nyei + N** = “the N that …”. Put the modifying clause before the noun.
10. Use **dongh + clause** after a noun for a looser, non-restrictive/appositive relative meaning.
11. **nyei** is multifunctional: it can mark possession, relative clauses, adverbial-like modification, or sentence-final assertive/stative meaning. Read it from position, not from one fixed gloss.
12. Numerals normally require classifiers: **NUM + CLF + N**. Common classifiers are **norm** (general objects / many time-place units), **dauh** (people/animals), **diuh** (long narrow things), and **laanh** (people in general); demonstratives such as **naaiv** “this” and **wuov** “that” often appear in **DEM + CLF + N** phrases, and **mbuo** marks plural with pronouns and some human nouns.

### Verbs and Predicate Types
13. Many English adjectives should be translated as stative verbs. Treat adjectival verbs as normal predicates and modifiers when appropriate.
14. One-argument clauses often use **NP + intransitive/stative verb**; two-argument clauses often use **NP + transitive verb + NP**. Example: *maa daic* “mother died”, *ninh nyanc hnaangx* “he eats rice”.
15. **maaih** means “have; there is/are”, **maiv maaih** means “there is no / do not have”, and **yiem** means “be at / stay / live in”. For equative/copular meanings, **se** is common for plain identity, while **benx** and **zeiz** have more specific uses (“be/become”, “be correct”).

### Aspect, Tense, and Modality
16. Tense is lexical, but aspect is grammaticalized. A common pattern is **Verb + postverbal aspect marker**.
17. **jienv** marks continuous/ongoing action; **jiex** marks experiential “have ever done”; **liuz** marks perfective/completed action, often with successful completion.
18. **baac** marks simple completion; **ziangx** marks a perfect-like past event with present relevance; **nzengc** marks “all used up”; **dorng** marks cessation/conclusion.
19. Sentence-final particles also mark aspect: **nyei** = assertive/stative, **aqv** = new situation / change of state, **mi’aqv** = telic/completed occurrence.
20. Common preverbal auxiliaries are **oix** “want to”, **haih** “be able to”, **gaamv** “dare to”, **kangv/nyunc** “be willing to”. Negate them by putting **maiv/mv** before the auxiliary.
21. **duqv** after a verb expresses attained possibility/result: “can, manage to, get to”. Use it for achieved ability rather than as a plain future marker.
22. **oix NP V** can mean “want NP to V”, while **maiv oix V** means “do not want to V”.

### Negation
23. The main negator is **maiv**, with colloquial contracted **mv**. Place it immediately before the verb, stative verb, auxiliary, or coverb phrase being negated.
24. Basic negation pattern: **Subject + maiv/mv + Verb**. Negate auxiliaries the same way: **maiv haih**, **maiv oix**, **maiv kangv**.
25. In coverb constructions, negation goes before the whole coverb phrase, not between the coverb phrase and the main verb.
26. Negative imperative uses **maiv dungx / maiv dungv / mv dungv + V**: “Don’t V.” A softer prohibition can add **tov** or polite **oc**.
27. Double negation does not always cancel. Negating both an auxiliary and a result/main verb can still yield a stronger negative, while a fronted negative topic plus negative focus can yield a strong affirmative reading.

### Questions
28. Yes/no questions usually keep statement word order and add sentence-final **fai** with rising intonation. A falling question can use **saah/saaqh**.
29. Content questions use interrogative words in normal clause positions. Important forms: **haaix dauh** “who?”, **haaix nyungc** “what/which kind?”, **haaix zanc** “when?”, **yiem haaix / haaix ndau** “where?”, **hnangv haaix nor** “how?”, **weic haaix diuc** or colloquial **zoux haiv nyungc** “why?”, **mbuoqc ziex** “how many?”.
30. Sentence-final **mv bei** adds “I wonder …” or self-questioning uncertainty.

### Multi-Verb Constructions
31. Iu Mien freely uses multi-verb strings without conjunctions. Preserve the sequence when translating: common patterns are **auxiliary + V**, **V + aspect marker**, **V + directional verb**, **V + resultative complement**, and **coverb phrase + main VP**.
32. Resultative serial verbs use **V1 + V2** where V2 gives the result of V1 (e.g. *mborqv daic* “beat to death” vs. *mborqv daix* “kill by beating”), and coverb constructions follow **Topic + Coverb Phrase + Main Verb Phrase** with common coverbs such as **longc** “use”, **yiem** “from/at”, **gan** “follow/along”, and **bun** “give; let/for”.

### Clause Linking
33. Juxtaposition is common: two clauses side by side may imply sequence, condition, cause, or background when context makes the relation clear. Clause-level coordination commonly uses **yaac** “and/also”, **mv.baac** “but”, and **fai** “or”, while **caux** is mainly NP-level “and/with”.
34. A standard conditional is **se.gorngv ... nor** = “if ...”. Other useful linkers are **laaix + clause** “because of / due to”, **maiv.gunv / mv.gunv + clause** “although / even though”, **weic + clause** “in order that / so that”, **weic.zuqc + clause** “because”, **... wuov.zanc** “when”, **... nyei ziangh.hoc** “at the time when”, and **hnangv ... wuov.nor** “as if / just like”; complement clauses usually have no separate word for English “that”, and verbs such as **gorngv** can be followed directly by the content clause.
